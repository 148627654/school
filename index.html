series overview

The ACP Summer School series was spear-headed by the Association for Constraint Programming (CP) as a means to augment and complement university teaching of CP, and to disseminate a core body of CP knowledge supporting the recognition of CP as a mature and relevant technology for use in industry. ACP Summer School 2026 will be hosted by the Jilin University and held in Changchun, China, during August 24-28, 2026.

2026 program

The Summer School, themed “CP: Explanation, Verification and Applications”, will have eight international experts to provide a general introduction to CP and applications in addition to specialised topics. Learning activities include lectures and hands-on sessions. A hackathon will also be organised allowing learners to further practise their skills. There will also be Teaching Assistants to guide the learners and enhance their learning experience.

target audience

PhD and Masters students, young PhD graduates and industrial practitioners who want to gain a good understanding of CP and its current research directions are all welcome. Prior knowledge of CP is not assumed, but general problem-solving skills, good programming and mathematical know-how are useful.

our speakers

Peter J. Stuckey
Monash University
Australia

Peter J. Stuckey

Title and official affiliation: Professor, Monash University

Topic: Modelling Discrete Optimisation Problems

Bio: Peter J. Stuckey is a Professor in the Department of Data Science and Artificial Intelligence in the Faculty of Information Technology at Monash University. He is a pioneer in constraint programming, the science of modelling and solving complex combinatorial problems. His research interests include: discrete optimization; programming languages, in particular declarative programming languages; constraint solving algorithms; path finding; bioinformatics; and constraint-based graphics. He enjoys problem-solving in many areas, having published in, e.g., databases, election science, system security, and timetabling, and having worked with companies such as Oracle and Rio Tinto on problems of interest to them.

Peter J. Stuckey received a B.Sc. and a Ph.D., both in Computer Science, from Monash University in 1985 and 1988, respectively. In 2009, he was recognized as an ACM Distinguished Scientist. In 2010, he was awarded the Google Australia Eureka Prize for Innovation in Computer Science for his work on lazy clause generation, and the University of Melbourne Woodward Medal for the most outstanding publication in Science and Technology across the university. In 2019, he was elected an AAAI Fellow and awarded the Association for Constraint Programming Award for Research Excellence.

Guido Tack
Monash University
Australia

Guido Tack

Title and official affiliation: Associate Professor, Monash University

Topic: Fundamentals of Constraint Programming Solvers

Bio: Guido Tack's research focuses on combinatorial optimisation, in particular on the architecture and implementation techniques for constraint solvers, the translation of constraint modelling languages, and industrial applications. He leads the development of the MiniZinc constraint modelling language and toolchain, and is one of the main developers of the Gecode constraint programming library. His broader research interests include programming languages and computational logic.

Alexey Ignatiev
Monash University
Australia

Alexey Ignatiev

Title and official affiliation: Associate Professor, Monash University

Topic: Efficient Computing with SAT

Bio: Alexey Ignatiev is an Associate Professor in the Optimisation Research Group of the Faculty of Information Technology at Monash University, Melbourne. His research lies at the intersection of propositional satisfiability (SAT), satisfiability modulo theories (SMT), and artificial intelligence, with a focus on developing efficient reasoning and optimization algorithms. Before joining Monash, he worked with Joao Marques-Silva at the University of Lisbon, Portugal, advancing methods for reasoning with SAT oracles. He completed his PhD at the Matrosov Institute for System Dynamics and Control Theory of the Russian Academy of Sciences, where he studied the integration of conflict-driven clause learning with binary decision diagrams. Alexey has developed state-of-the-art solving technology, including the award-winning MaxSAT solver RC2 and the widely adopted PySAT toolkit — which has become a core component of the Python ecosystem for SAT-based problem solving. His recent work has also helped establish the foundations of formal explainable AI (FXAI), defining formal abductive and contrastive explanations for machine learning (ML) models and introducing efficient algorithms for their computation and enumeration.

Emir Demirović
Delft University of Technology, Netherlands

Emir Demirović

Title and official affiliation: Associate Professor, TU Delft

Topic: LCG Solving

Bio: Emir Demirović is an associate professor of computer science at TU Delft (Netherlands), where he leads the Constraint Solving ("ConSol") Research Group and directs the Explainable AI in Transportation ("XAIT") Lab. He has been recognized with the Early Career Researcher Award 2025 from the Association for Constraint Programming and is an ELLIS Scholar. His research focuses on exploiting structural properties of NP-hard problems to design algorithms that are both theoretically complete and efficient in practice, with a particular emphasis on constraint programming and dynamic programming techniques. His techniques have advanced state-of-the-art solvers in MaxSAT and constraint programming — achieving high rankings in competitions such as the MaxSAT Evaluation and the MiniZinc Challenge — his optimal decision tree methods (in machine learning) are among the fastest, and his recent approach to certifying outputs of constraint programming solvers on large-scale problems has been highlighted by Donald Knuth in The Art of Computer Programming (Vol. 4, Fasc. 7) as a promising advancement.

Tias Guns
KU Leuven
Belgium

Tias Guns

Title and official affiliation: Professor, KU Leuven

Topic: Explainable Constraint Programming

Bio: Tias Guns is a professor in computer science at the DTAI Lab of KU Leuven, Belgium. His research lies at the intersection of combinatorial optimisation and machine learning. In 2021, he was awarded a prestigious 5-year ERC Consolidator Grant for "Conversational Human-aware Technologies for Optimisation". He has organized numerous workshops and tutorials on explainable constraint solving, and his CP25 paper "Modeling and Explaining an Industrial Workforce Allocation and Scheduling Problem" won the Best Application Paper Award. He is eager to share insights and techniques on this topic at the summer school.

Cieran McCreesh
University of Glasgow
UK

Ciaran McCreesh

Title and official affiliation: Royal Academy of Engineering Research Fellow, University of Glasgow

Topic: Proof Logging

Bio: Ciaran McCreesh’s research focuses on subgraph discovery — using algorithms to identify smaller graphs within larger ones. To achieve this, he combines concepts from constraint programming and Boolean satisfiability with techniques in algorithm engineering and high-performance computing. In recent years, his primary focus has been on ensuring the correctness of complex algorithm implementations: he will introduce a technique known as "proof logging", where solvers output not just a solution, but also an independently verifiable mathematical proof of its validity.

Helmut Simonis
University College Cork
UK

Helmut Simonis

Title and official affiliation: Senior Research Fellow, University College Cork

Topic: Applications, Real-world Examples and Success Stories

Bio: Helmut Simonis has been an internationally recognized expert in Constraint Programming since 1986. He has led industrial research projects across Europe, co-founded COSYTEC SA in Paris, France, and has been a key researcher at University College Cork (UCC) since 2008. He has received numerous awards for his contributions to scheduling and artificial intelligence (AI), holds four patents currently owned by Cisco Systems, and brings decades of experience training organizations to implement AI-powered scheduling solutions.

Allen Z. Zhong
Monash University
Australia

About Allen Z. Zhong

Title and official affiliation: Lecturer, Monash University

Topic: Hackathon

Bio: Allen Z. Zhong is a lecturer in the Optimization Group within the Faculty of Information Technology at Monash University. He obtained his Ph.D. from The Chinese University of Hong Kong in 2023, under the supervision of Prof. Jimmy Lee. His research interests span the integration of optimization and machine learning, with a focus on developing model analysis techniques to detect and exploit dominance in constraint optimization and satisfaction problems. Additionally, he explores the combination of machine learning and optimization algorithms, aiming to develop robust, adaptive solutions for addressing real-world optimization challenges through interdisciplinary approaches.

Local Information

Weather

In August, Changchun’s average daily temperature ranges from 19°C to 28°C, with a day–night difference of about 8–10°C. Days are generally mild, while nights can feel slightly cool, so bringing a light jacket is recommended. Participants are also advised to bring an umbrella depending on the weather. Air quality is consistently excellent, making it well suited for outdoor activities.

How to get to China (Changchun)

International travel to China

Most international participants from Australia, Canada, the United States, the United Kingdom, Belgium, Spain, and other regions will first arrive at a major international gateway city in China, such as Beijing, Shanghai, Guangzhou, Shenzhen, Xiamen, or Chengdu. From there, travelers will take a domestic flight to Changchun Longjia International Airport (CGQ, Changchun’s main international airport).

  • Major international hubs include:
    Beijing Capital International Airport (PEK) / Beijing Daxing International Airport (PKX)
    Shanghai Pudong International Airport (PVG) / Shanghai Hongqiao International Airport (SHA)
    Guangzhou Baiyun International Airport (CAN)
    Shenzhen Bao’an International Airport (SZX)
    Xiamen Gaoqi International Airport (XMN)
    Chengdu Tianfu International Airport (TFU) / Chengdu Shuangliu International Airport (CTU)
  • Changchun (CGQ) has frequent domestic flight connections from all major Chinese hubs

Typical travel routes

Common travel routes include:

  • North America / Europe → Beijing, Shanghai, or Guangzhou → Changchun (CGQ)
  • Australia → Beijing / Shanghai / Guangzhou / Shenzhen → Changchun (CGQ)

Most routes involve one international flight followed by one domestic transfer within China.

Transfer tips (within China)

When transferring from an international flight to a domestic flight within China, travelers should allow sufficient time for arrival and transfer procedures.

  • Recommended transfer time: 2–3 hours
  • If checked baggage is through-checked to Changchun, a 2-hour transfer is usually sufficient
  • If baggage must be claimed and re-checked, additional time may be required
  • Large hub airports may involve long walking distances or terminal changes

Arrival in Changchun

Changchun Longjia International Airport (CGQ) is the main airport serving Changchun city.

  • Flight time from Beijing: approximately 2 hours
  • Flight time from Shanghai: approximately 3 hours
  • Airport to city center: approximately 30–45 minutes by taxi or airport shuttle

Visa & Entry notice

Participants should check the latest Chinese visa and transit policies applicable to their nationality before departure.

  • Some nationalities may qualify for visa-free transit under specific conditions
  • Entry requirements may change; please consult the nearest Chinese embassy or official government sources
