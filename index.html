About the Summer School

ACP Summer School is committed to creating an inclusive environment. Please refer to our Duty of Respect and Equal Opportunity Policy.

series overview

The ACP Summer School series was spear-headed by the Association for Constraint Programming (CP) as a means to augment and complement university teaching of CP, and to disseminate a core body of CP knowledge supporting the recognition of CP as a mature and relevant technology for use in industry. ACP Summer School 2026 will be hosted by the Jilin University and held in Changchun, China, during August 24-28, 2026.

2026 program

The Summer School, themed ‚ÄúCP: Explanation, Verification and Applications‚Äù, will have eight international experts to provide a general introduction to CP and applications in addition to specialised topics. Learning activities include lectures and hands-on sessions. A hackathon will also be organised allowing learners to further practise their skills. There will also be Teaching Assistants to guide the learners and enhance their learning experience.

target audience

PhD and Masters students, young PhD graduates and industrial practitioners who want to gain a good understanding of CP and its current research directions are all welcome. Prior knowledge of CP is not assumed, but general problem-solving skills, good programming and mathematical know-how are useful.

Alexey Ignatiev
Monash University
Australia

Alexey Ignatiev

Title and official affiliation: Associate Professor, Monash¬†University

Topic: Efficient Computing with SAT

Bio: Alexey Ignatiev is an Associate Professor in the Optimisation research group of the Faculty of Information Technology at Monash University, Melbourne. His research lies at the intersection of propositional satisfiability (SAT), satisfiability modulo theories (SMT), and artificial intelligence, with a focus on developing efficient reasoning and optimization algorithms. Before joining Monash, he worked with Joao Marques-Silva and at the University of Lisbon, Portugal, advancing methods for reasoning with SAT oracles. He did his PhD at the Matrosov Institute for System Dynamics and Control Theory of the Russian Academy of Sciences, where he studied the integration of conflict-driven clause learning with binary decision diagrams. Alexey has authored state-of-the-art solving technology, including the award-winning MaxSAT solver RC2 and the widely adopted PySAT toolkit, which has become a core component of the Python ecosystem for SAT-based problem solving. His recent work also helped establish the foundations of formal explainable AI (FXAI), defining formal abductive and contrastive explanations for machine learning (ML) models and introducing efficient algorithms for their computation and enumeration.

Topic: LCG Solving

Bio: Emir Demiroviƒá is an associate professor of computer science at TU Delft (Netherlands), where he leads the Constraint Solving ("ConSol") research group and directs the Explainable AI in Transportation ("XAIT") lab. Cieran McCreesh
University of Glasgow
UK

Ciaran McCreesh

Title and official affiliation: Royal Academy of Engineering Research Fellow, University of Glasgow

Topic: proof logging

Bio: A simple writeup to introduce themselves: Ciaran McCreesh is interested in using algorithms to find little graphs inside bigger graphs. To do this, he combines ideas taken from constraint programming and Boolean satisfiability with techniques from algorithm engineering and high performance computing. Recently, his main focus has been in working out how we can be sure that complex algorithm implementations are giving the right answer: he will introduce a technique known as "proof logging", where solvers output not just a solution, but also an independently verifiable mathematical proof of its correctness.
